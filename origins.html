I–III. Introduction, Origins & Early Durhamstown Settlement (c.1730–1857)

Origins. The Keary / Kearey surname descends from Ó Ciardha, of the southern Uí Néill. Settlement in Meath and Offaly persisted after twelfth‑century Anglo‑Norman incursions, with eighteenth‑century parish concentrations around Navan (Martry, Ardbraccan). The reconstructed line begins with Luke Keary of Martry (d. 1806), evidenced by a highly legible headstone that also names his wife Elizabeth (alias Rooney), their daughter Anne, and the son Thomas who commissioned the stone and died in 1857 (¹).

Thomas of Liscarton. By Griffith’s Valuation (c.1854), Thomas Keary holds a house, offices, and c.13½ acres at Liscarton from the Earl of Essex (Winter Estate) (²). The Valuation Office Revision Books record the marginal transfer “Dead → to James Keary,” proving a direct father‑to‑son succession (³). O’Hart situates Thomas as son of a Luke of Skreen, naming sons Luke, Hugh, James—a pattern reflected across later registers.

1821 Census households. Ardbraccan/Dormstown shows multiple Keary entries, notably a unit headed by Thomas (50) with Anne (35) and children Luke (7), Thomas (0), Anne (5), Bridget (2). The names and locale anticipate the nineteenth‑century chain, reinforcing geographic continuity despite the gaps in early civil registration.

Context. Estate consolidation and cotter rationalisation shaped tenancy options; Catholic emancipation advanced slowly; tithe conflict and Famine shocks compelled households to mix farming with herding, day‑labour, and service. The evidence shows the Kearys retained a stable holding, invested in literacy (epigraphy), and maintained kin proximity to Navan’s markets and chapels.
