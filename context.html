IX. Socioeconomic, Religious, and Cultural Commentary & References

Tenure and reform. The Keary holdings in Liscarton/Durhamstown traversed the arc from leasehold under the Essex estate to the era of the Irish Land Acts (1870–1909). Surviving valuation annotations (“Dead → to James”) exemplify how Catholic tenant families transferred use rights within the constraints and opportunities of estate oversight (³⁰).

Religion and register survival. Mixed denominational footprints—COI (St Paul’s) and RC (St Mary’s)—in 1791 Dublin baptisms are not contradictory but typical of pragmatic sacramental strategies in a period of overlapping identities and mobility (³¹).

Urban crafts. The London offshoot’s movement into goldworking connects to Soho’s nineteenth‑century metal trades and to broader artisan economies that absorbed Irish migrants. The orthographic shift Keary → Kearey aligns with registry practice and prestige spelling in trade documentation (³²).

Enumeration. The 1821, 1901, and 1911 returns situate the Kearys within the Ardbraccan–Navan–Kells corridor; the Foley/Polian households illustrate fosterage/niece arrangements typical in rural‑urban interfaces (³³).
