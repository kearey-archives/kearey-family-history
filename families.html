VI. Associated Families and Neighbour Networks (1900 – 1930)

1 The Leonard Connection of Ongenstown

The Leonards appear frequently beside the Kearys in parish and civil records from the 1880s. The earliest securely identified link is the 1886/7 marriage of Luke Keary and Mary Leonard:

Marriage: Luke Keary (35), bachelor, farmer, of Ongenstown; father James Keary (farmer). Mary Leonard (25), spinster, of Ongenstown; father Nicholas Leonard (farmer). Witnesses Patrick Keary and Mary Leonard. (Civil Marriage Record, 1886/7)1

Mary’s birth certificate (1871) lists her father as Nicholas Leonard and mother Anne Reilly.2 Her age at marriage (about 25) aligns closely with this record, demonstrating ordinary registration variance of ± 3 years — typical when literacy was limited and ages estimated by family memory.

Mary’s death certificate (1935) records: “Mary Keary, widow, aged 74, of Ongenstown; informant Patrick Leonard, brother.”3 This confirms that she was a Leonard by birth and that her brother Patrick remained nearby, sustaining intermarriage between neighbouring families in the Ardbraccan district.

Economic context. The Leonards were smallholders and dairy farmers, part of the “strong farmer” class that prospered under the post-1881 Land Acts. Their alliance with the Kearys created a network of medium-sized holdings controlling roughly 30–40 acres across Ongenstown and Durhamstown — sufficient to sustain families without subletting, but small enough to require the labour of women and children.

2 The Foley Connection of Kilmessan and Rathfeigh

The Foley family, later linked through Rose Foley, appears in both civil and census records. Rose married a Keary of the prior generation (Luke) and became mother of Christopher Keary (b. 1866). Her death certificate states: “Rose Keary, widow of a farmer, aged 82, died 1921 at Ongenstown; informant Christy Keary, son.”4

The 1901 Census of Kilmessan lists another branch:

Residents of house 18, Kilmessan (Meath)
Francis Foley   45  Head  Farmer
Bridget Foley   30  Wife
William         10  Son
Julia            8  Daughter
Maggie           6  Daughter
Mary             4  Daughter
Jane             1  Daughter
Margaret        73  Mother
(Roman Catholic)

(National Archives of Ireland, 1901 Kilmessan return.)5

The youngest, Jane Foley, is crucial: she later appears as Jane Foley aged 11 in the 1911 census of Edoxtown (Rathfeigh), residing with her aunt and uncle Peter and Jane Polian, recorded as niece.6 Jane ultimately married and was widowed; her death certificate (1969/70) notes Kathleen Martin, niece, Slane Road, as informant,7 tying the Foleys into the later Martin family of Bohermeen.

3 The Polian and Magrane Households (1911)

The 1911 Census, house 4 in Edoxtown (Rathfeigh), records:

Peter Polian    58  Head of Family  Roman Catholic
Jane Polian     56  Wife            Roman Catholic
Jane Foley      11  Niece           Roman Catholic
James Magrane   74  Uncle           Roman Catholic

This small rural household shows the intergenerational pattern of care typical of Catholic kinship: widowed or orphaned relatives (Jane Foley) were absorbed by elder kin (the Polians). The Magrane connection links back to farm-labour networks around Kilmessan, and the whole cluster demonstrates the durable Meath custom of neighbour-kin reciprocity, an informal social welfare system prior to the Free State’s public assistance acts.

4 The Martin Connection and Final Bohermeen Burials

By mid-twentieth century the Kearys were represented chiefly by Jane Foley (née Keary) and her Martin relations. Jane’s 1969/70 death record identifies Kathleen Martin, niece, Slane Road, as informant.7 Bohermeen church registers and the modern cemetery survey record adjacent Martin and Keary graves, implying continuing kin contact into the 1970s; specifically, St Ultan’s, Plot B-294, lists Anne (1943), Christopher (1945), Thomas (1954) and Jane (1969).8

The social meaning of such proximity is significant: burial adjacency among related female-line descendants (Mullon, Martin, Foley) reflects enduring matrilineal memory even as the Keary surname waned locally.
