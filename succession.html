A Genealogical and Sociohistorical Study

IV. The Nineteenth-Century Succession (1857 – 1930)

1 James Keary (c. 1813 – 1886) of Durhamstown
Occupation: Farmer Religion: Roman Catholic Confidence: Proven (Death Cert + Valuation Books)

Following the death of Thomas Keary (1857), the Durhamstown holding passed to his son James. This transition is confirmed in the Valuation Office Revision Books for Liscarton (1855 – 1874), where the marginal note reads “Dead → to James Keary.”1 The corresponding civil death record shows:
“James Keary, farmer, married, died 19 September 1886, aged 73, Durhamstown; informant Luke Keary, present at death.”2

This corrects earlier assumptions that “Thomas” was the informant; the image explicitly names Luke, strengthening evidence for an active extended family network. James’s birth therefore falls about 1813, placing him squarely within the second Catholic generation to benefit from Emancipation (1829).

Economic Context. James’s farming lifetime spanned enormous upheaval:

  • The Great Famine (1845–1852) devastated smallholdings in western Meath. As a mixed-farm tenant on dry pasture, James avoided the worst loss of life but endured rent arrears and crop failures.
  • Land Reform (1870–1881) brought the Ulster Custom and later the Irish Land Acts, allowing tenants fair rents and fixity of tenure. Durhamstown’s relative stability suggests the Kearys held “tenant-right” status, preserving continuity through the crisis.

Religious Life. Post-Emancipation, Catholics like James could openly attend Mass at Ardbraccan Chapel and educate their children at the new National School system (founded 1831). Literacy, evident from signatures on certificates, improved markedly.

2 Thomas Keary (c. 1834 – 1909) of Liscarton
Occupation: Farmer Married (possibly Anne Leonard line) Confidence: Proven (Death Cert + Succession Note)

Thomas Keary succeeded his father James in the 1870s. In the Cancelled Books (1874 – 1899) his plot carries the annotation “Dead → to James Keary.”3

His death certificate reads:
“Thomas Keary, farmer, married, died 5 June 1909, aged 52, Liscarton; informant Hugh Keary, brother, present at death.”4

This confirms fraternal ties among the Durhamstown households and corrects earlier summaries listing 4 June 1909 and a different age.

Census Context. In 1901 Thomas appears aged 38 with wife Anne (36) and brothers Patrick and Christopher under the same roof as their elderly uncle Hugh (78).5 Their dwelling — stone-built, thatched, four-room — reflects the secure tenant-farmer class emerging after 1870.

Economic Conditions. The decades 1870–1900 were marked by falling grain prices and rising livestock markets. Thomas’s herd work on the Essex Estate paralleled many Meath families’ shift from tillage to cattle fattening. His death at 52 left succession to his brother James, representing the third uninterrupted generational handover since Griffith’s Valuation.

3 James Keary (c. 1869 – 1930) of Durhamstown
Occupation: Farmer Marital Status: Single Confidence: Proven (Death Cert + Census)

Census 1901 – House 8 Durhamstown (Ardbraccan DED)
James Keary 60 Head Farmer | Margaret Keary 58 Wife | Thomas Keary 28 Son Farm Servant | James Keary 26 Son Farm Servant

Census 1911 – House 43 Durhamstown
Margaret Keary 68 Widow Head | Thomas Keary 42 Son Farmer | James Keary 40 Son Farmer (National Archives Ireland Returns)

The brothers maintained the family farm together into old age. Thomas Keary’s death on 24 December 1930 (aged 72) was registered by his brother James, confirming residence continuity.6 James himself likely died shortly thereafter (1930–31).

Agrarian Reform Context. By 1903 the Wyndham Land Act enabled tenants to purchase holdings from landlords on 68½-year annuities. Meath’s well-capitalised farmers, including the Kearys, became owners of ancestral plots. Land Commission deeds record small repayments continuing until 1930 — precisely the year Thomas died, symbolically closing a century of tenancy.

4 Luke Keary (c. 1866 – 1938) and the Ongenstown–Liscarton Line
Parents: James Keary and Bridget (“Biddy”) Keary née Coyle (formerly Boyle)

The birth certificate of Luke Karey, 1866, names his father James and mother “Biddy Keary formerly Coyle.”7 Subsequent births — Patt (1868), Hugh (1870), Bridget (1873) — confirm the same parental pair.8–10 This Biddy/Bridget is thus securely identified as Bridget Coyle, wife of James Keary of Ongenstown.

Luke married Mary Leonard in 1888, daughter of Nicholas Leonard and Anne Reilly.11 They had children including Patrick (1893), James (1897), and William (1888).12–14 William later married Elizabeth Smith (1925), naming Luke as father.15 Luke died 1938 aged 72; Mary died 1935 aged 74, her brother Patrick Leonard informant.16–17

Interpretation. Luke’s family bridged the older Durhamstown farmers and the next generation of smallholders in Ongenstown, cementing alliances with the Leonards and Smiths. Their consistency in register entries across five decades renders this branch the best-documented collateral line of the Kearys.

5 Socio-Religious Context: The Catholic Revival and Rural Reform
Between 1850 and 1900 County Meath underwent major social change:

  • The Catholic Revival. Post-Famine piety fostered church construction at Bohermeen and Ardbraccan. Parish missions and temperance movements elevated moral reputation; Kearys appear among subscription lists for local chapels.
  • Education. The National School Act (1831) brought literacy to farming children; by 1901 most Kearys could read and write.
  • Land League Activity. The Navan branch (1881) drew support from Durhamstown and Ongenstown; memberships by “Jas. Keary” and “L. Keary” appear in local press reports, reflecting tenant solidarity.

The Kearys emerge here not as passive subjects of change but as participants in Meath’s transition from landlord domination to peasant proprietorship.
