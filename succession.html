IV–V. Nineteenth‑Century Succession & Collateral Branches (1857–1930)

James of Durhamstown (†1886). The civil death register records James Keary, farmer of Durhamstown, died 19 September 1886, age recorded as 73, with informant Luke Keary present (⁵). This clarifies two points: (a) James is the immediate successor to Thomas per the Revision Book, and (b) the informant Luke anchors a collateral sub‑line active in the following generation.

Thomas of Liscarton (†1909). The death of Thomas Keary on 5 June 1909 (registered age 52 on the certificate presently cited by the contributor; earlier readings placed him at 71) is recorded at Liscarton, occupation “herd,” with informant Hugh Keary, brother (⁶). This indicates a sibling support network and suggests intra‑family labour specialisation (herding) within the estate economy.

Thomas of Durhamstown (†1930). Another Thomas Keary, farmer, died 24 December 1930, age 72, Durhamstown; informant Christopher Keary, brother (⁷). The informant name “Christopher” recurs in later records and in the Bohermeen cluster, stitching the nineteenth‑century succession to twentieth‑century collateral households.

Hugh and Christopher. Hugh Keary of Durhamstown died 13 February 1907, age 83; informant Annie Keary, niece (⁸). Another Hugh (Betaghstown) died 25 January 1943, age 72; informant Mrs Annie Mullon, niece (⁹). Christopher Keary died 16 August 1945, age 80; informant Joseph O’Byrne, occupier (¹⁰). Repetition of the niece “Annie”—appearing pre‑marriage as Keary and post‑marriage as Mullon/Mullins—links the Durhamstown and Betaghstown households and culminates in the shared grave at Bohermeen.

Bohermeen Plot B‑294. St Ultan’s, Bohermeen (IrishGraveyards register) lists Anne (Annie) Keary (Feb 1943), Christopher Keary (Aug 1945), Thomas Keary (Aug 1954), and Jane Keary (Oct 1969) in the same plot, confirming a durable household nexus across the 1940s–1960s (¹¹).
