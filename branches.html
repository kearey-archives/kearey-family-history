V. Collateral Branches and Later Generations (1900 – 1930)

1 Hugh Keary (c. 1824 – 1907)
Occupation: Farmer Confidence: Proven (Death Cert)

“Hugh Keary, farmer, married, died 25 January 1907, aged 83, Durhamstown; informant Christopher Keary, nephew, present at death.”1

This record, combined with the presence of a Christopher Keary in later documents, confirms Hugh as an elder brother of James (1813 – 1886). His longevity links the Martry and Ongenstown clusters, marking the culmination of the original eighteenth-century generation.

2 Christopher Keary (1866 – 1945) and Anne (Annie) Keary (1870 – 1943)

Christopher’s birth record (1866) lists Luke Keary and Rose Foley as parents.2 Rose died 1921 aged 82, “Christy Keary son” informant.3 Thus, Christopher of Durhamstown and “Christy Keary of Betaghstown” are identical.

Death Records. Anne Keary died Feb 1943; Christopher died 16 Aug 1945 aged 80, informant Joseph O’Byrne (occupier).4 Both rest in St Ultan’s Churchyard, Bohermeen, with later burials of Thomas (1954) and Jane (1969) in the same plot.5

Interpretation. This Bohermeen grave unites three branches: the Durhamstown Kearys, the Foleys, and the Mullon/Martin kin. Annie’s recurrence as “niece” informant (1907 Hugh; 1943 Christopher) cements her role as family caretaker during the twilight of the Meath line.

3 Economic and Social Transition (1900 – 1930)

By 1900 Meath was one of Ireland’s wealthiest agricultural counties. The Kearys benefited from livestock trade, rail access to Dublin, and local co-operatives. After independence (1922) they experienced relative prosperity under the Free State’s Department of Agriculture grants. Yet the old households aged; younger members sought urban employment or married into town families. By the 1930s only a few Kearys remained on ancestral acres.
