I–III. Introduction, Origins & Early Durhamstown Settlement (c.1730–1857)

Origins of the name. Keary / Kearey descends from the Gaelic Ó Ciardha, ‘descendant of Ciardha’ (the ‘dark‑haired’), a southern Uí Néill lineage whose medieval homeland lay across modern Meath/Offaly. By the eighteenth century, occupational migration and estate rationalisation drew Ó Ciardha families into parishes around Navan, including Martry and Ardbraccan, where Catholic tenant households clustered beneath large Protestant‑owned estates.

Earliest anchor: Luke of Martry. A carved headstone at Martry (Kells) records Luke Keary (d. 24 Oct 1806, aged 75), his wife Elizabeth (alias Rooney; d. 15 Feb 1813, 87), their daughter Anne (d. 24 Apr 1800, 28), and the son who erected the stone, Thomas Keary (d. 18 Jan 1857, 91), with his wife Anne (d. 31 Aug 1804, 82). The artistry and explicit kin terms imply literacy and modest prosperity, and—critically—identify Thomas as the next bearer of the tenancy line (¹).

Thomas at Liscarton. Mid‑century records place Thomas Keary as tenant of a house, offices and c.13½ acres at Liscarton under the Earl of Essex (Winter Estate), as per Griffith’s Valuation (c.1854). Subsequent Valuation Office Revision Books note the marginal succession “Dead → to James Keary,” establishing the father‑to‑son transfer of the holding (²)(³).

1821 Census resonance. The 1821 Ardbraccan returns capture several Keary households at Dormstown (Durhamstown), including a family headed by Thomas (50) with wife Anne (35) and children Luke (7), Thomas (0), Anne (5), and Bridget (2). Although early enumeration has gaps, the given forenames and location coherently anticipate the nineteenth‑century documentary chain.

Context. The move Martry → Durhamstown reflects estate consolidation and the search for stable leases. Catholic emancipation advanced unevenly; tithe agitation and Famine shocks pressed tenant families to diversify labour (herding, seasonal hire) yet also to invest in durable kin networks around Navan chapels and markets.
