IX. Economic, Religious, and Cultural Commentary (1730–1969): What Was Happening When Our People Lived It

Tenure & Reform: From Essex estate tenancy to Land Acts, the Liscarton holding illustrates Catholic resilience and incremental security; valuation margins (“Dead → to James”) are classic administrative fingerprints of transfer.

Religious Footprints: Dual COI/RC registrations around 1791 reflect jurisdictional pragmatism among migrants; rural Meath practice remained predominantly RC.

Labour Spectrum: Herding, outdoor service, flax, and later London metal trades formed a coherent survivability strategy attuned to market cycles and apprenticeship opportunities.

Kinship Care: Niece informants (Annie Mullon; Jane Foley) and shared plots (Bohermeen B‑294) illuminate care and co‑residence structures enduring into the 1940s–60s.
