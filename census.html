VII. The Meath Census Returns (1821 – 1911) and Family Distribution

1 The 1821 Census as Genealogical Baseline

The 1821 enumeration for Martry and Ardbraccan preserves more than forty Keary entries. Three households are pivotal:

  • House 108, Dormstown (Ardbraccan) — Thomas (50), Anne (35), Luke (7), Thomas (0), Anne (5), Bridget (2), two servants → the core Durhamstown line.
  • Houses 6 & 12, Castlemartin (Martry) — two inter-related Keary families headed by Thomas (30) and John (50) → probable collaterals.
  • House 14, Martry village — John (66), Patt (40), William (30), James (20) → earlier Martry branch.

Cross-referencing these with the gravestone of Luke (†1806) confirms continuity from Martry to Durhamstown over three generations.

3 Social and Economic Interpretation (1900 – 1911)

Population stability. Despite mass emigration elsewhere, Meath’s population fell less drastically than Connacht’s; families like the Kearys benefited from fertile land and cattle grazing.

Religious life. All censuses record Roman Catholic affiliation and English-speaking literacy, signifying integration within the post-Emancipation Catholic middle strata.

Housing quality. Most Keary houses were Category 2 or 3 (stone walls, thatched roofs, 3–4 rooms) — a step above labourers yet below gentry farmhouses, typical for tenant-owners under the Essex Estate.

Labour and gender. Women (e.g., Anne Keary flax-spinner in 1821) participated in subsistence industry until mechanised linen collapsed by 1890; afterward, domestic service and teaching became common occupations for daughters.

Community interlinkage. Frequent witnessing of baptisms and marriages across Keary, Leonard, Foley, and Polian families illustrates dense inter-kin bonds typical of Meath’s rural Catholic society — a key factor in the family’s longevity and social resilience.
