VIII. The Dublin & London Offshoot (1791–Present): Migration, Anglicisation, and Social Mobility

Bridge via Dublin, 1791. Two 1791 Dublin baptisms for boys named Thomas Keary appear in parish registers: at St Paul’s (Church of Ireland), parents James & Mary (²⁵); and at St Mary’s Pro‑Cathedral (RC), parents James Keary & Catherine Byrne, sponsors James Dardis & Jane Nowlan (²⁶). The London line demonstrably descends from the St Paul’s entry, given later Anglican rites and metropolitan records.

Emigration and trade. Thomas Kearey removes to London c.1816, marrying Hester (Esther) Pepler at St Anne, Soho (1819). Occupations range across early‑Victorian urban crafts: baggage porter, gold refiner, and goldsmith, reflecting porous boundaries between metal trades and transport labour (²⁷). The surname spelling stabilises as Kearey in London sources—a not uncommon anglicisation consistent with phonetic rendering and guild/registration practice (²⁸).

Line of descent. Thomas Kearey (bapt. 1791) → Thomas Henry Kearey (1820–1867) m. Hannah Raybould (1823–1878) → Alfred Kearey (1854–1917) m. Martha Sutton (1857–1947) → Albert Edward Kearey (1889–1971; Royal Engineers 1914–1920) m. Elsie May Collins (1908–1969) → Terence Arthur Kearey (b. 1935). The family cluster is anchored in Paddington/Kensington and later Harrow.

War losses. Alfred Kearey and several sons served in the First World War; Alfred and three sons—Alfred Ernest, Frederick Augustus, and Sidney Reginald—were killed, a grievous contraction for a skilled artisan household (²⁹).

Interpretation. The London branch illustrates how rural Irish surnames entered metropolitan registers through mixed‑confessional and occupational channels. The adoption of the Kearey spelling can be read as an administrative regularisation that nevertheless preserved the spoken name; its persistence into the twentieth century and into the compiler’s generation underscores the offshoot’s continuity.
