The Keary Family of Martry, Durhamstown, and Liscarton, County Meath (c.1730–1978)

A Genealogical and Sociohistorical Study

IX. Economic, Religious, and Cultural Commentary (1730–1978): What Was Happening When Our People Lived It

1. The Penal Era to Emancipation (c. 1730–1830)

Constraints and resilience. Families like the Kearys navigated land leases under Protestant estates (Essex/Winter). Despite legal disabilities, Catholics in Martry and Ardbraccan sustained parish worship (often in modest chapels), and hedge schools seeded the literacy later visible in headstones and signatures. Luke Keary’s carefully carved stone (1806) showcases this emergent Catholic respectability.

Markets and proto-industry. Meath’s flax premiums (1790s) and small-scale linen spinning provided cash income beside mixed tillage; a James Keary appears in contemporary flax lists from Martry. Tenant security here rested on reputation and rent reliability—qualities transmitted intergenerationally.

2. The Long Nineteenth Century (1830–1911): Famine, Reform, Purchase

Famine impact. Meath suffered less mortality than Connacht but endured grain failure, workhouse strain, and emigration. Surviving tenants (including the Kearys) consolidated acreage and pivoted toward cattle fattening, suited to Meath’s heavy soils.

The Land War and Acts. Agitation (1879–82) was followed by reform: the 1870 and 1881 Land Acts (fixity/fair rents), then the Wyndham Act 1903 (tenant purchase). By 1911 many Keary holdings were owner-occupied, with widow-and-son structures (e.g., Rose with Thomas and Christopher) characteristic of late transfer patterns.

Religion and education. Post-Emancipation Catholic parish life flourished; National Schools (after 1831) raised literacy, enabling younger Kearys (Luke, Hugh, James) to serve as informants, witnesses, and ratepayers—documentary fingerprints of civic competence.

3. The Free State Era and After (1922–1978): Stability and Closure

Agriculture. Interwar Meath maintained mixed dairying and beef; small owner-occupiers survived with family labour and co-operative marketing.

Demography. With urban migration and childless sibling households, the surname’s rural presence waned. The Bohermeen plot (B-294) binding Annie, Christopher, Thomas, and Jane is thus both a family memorial and a social fact: the closure of the Meath surname line even as female-line descendants (Foley, Martin, Mullen) continued locally.

Yet the Keary name itself persisted at Martry, where a later headstone commemorates Patrick Keary (1893–1955), James Keary (1897–1963), Mary Keary († 1967), Betty Keary (1888–1970), and William Keary (1888–1978)—the children of Luke Keary (1866–1938) and Mary Leonard (1871–1935). Their return to Martry closes the genealogical circle, uniting the eighteenth-century homeland with the final living generation of the Meath line.1

Memory. The twenty-first-century living line—through Terence Arthur Kearey (b. 1935)—keeps a continuous memory from Luke (†1806 Martry) to London present: two and a half centuries of adaptation across place and class.

Compiler’s Note

Irish civil records occasionally show variant spellings (e.g., Carey for Keary; Kearey in London); identification rests on place, informants/witnesses, and kin recurrence more than on orthography alone. Ages on Irish certificates often vary by ±5–10 years; where conflict arises, contemporary events (marriage/children) and informant identity carry greatest weight.
