IX. Socioeconomic, Religious, and Cultural Commentary & References

Tenure and reform. The Essex estate tenancy at Liscarton, passing Thomas → James, typifies Catholic persistence into the Land Acts era; marginal notes in the Revision Books (“Dead → to James”) are classic signatures of continuity during transfer.

Religion and record survival. Mixed COI/RC footprints in Dublin 1791 baptisms reflect pragmatic sacramental recourse, not contradiction, particularly among migrants navigating overlapping parish jurisdictions.

Labour spectrum. From herding (“herd” on the 1909 certificate) and outdoor service (1821 Dormstown) to London metal trades, the Keary/Kearey story is one of flexible skills anchored by kin support.

Fosterage and nieces. Recurrent niece informants (Annie Mullon/Mullins; Jane Foley) illustrate care networks typical of Meath families across the early to mid‑twentieth century.
