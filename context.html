IX. Socioeconomic, Religious, and Cultural Commentary & References

Tenure & reform. The Keary holding under the Essex estate exemplifies Catholic tenant persistence into the Land Acts era (1870–1909). The valuation note “Dead → to James Keary” is a textbook marginal succession marking continuity of occupancy during a period when many leases fractured.

Religious footprints. Mixed confessional records (COI and RC) in Dublin 1791 reflect pragmatic sacramental practice rather than identity rupture, especially for migrants moving between parish jurisdictions and occupational milieus.

Labour & mobility. Herding at Liscarton, outdoor service at Dormstown (1821), and London metal trades map a spectrum of skills in which kinship and reputation mediated opportunity.

Enumeration & care. The presence of nieces (e.g., Annie Mullon/Mullins; Jane Foley) as informants or household members points to robust care networks binding collateral branches into the mid‑twentieth century.
