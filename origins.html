I–III. Introduction, Origins & Early Durhamstown Settlement (c.1730–1857)

The surname Keary / Kearey derives from Ó Ciardha, the southern Uí Néill sept whose territory centred on Cairbre. By the long eighteenth century, Ó Ciardha households appear in Meath parish clusters around Navan, especially Martry and Ardbraccan, reflecting Catholic tenant strategies under large estates.

Luke of Martry. The Martry headstone records Luke Keary (d. 24 Oct 1806, 75), his wife Elizabeth (alias Rooney; d. 15 Feb 1813, 87), their daughter Anne (d. 24 Apr 1800, 28), and the son Thomas (d. 18 Jan 1857, 91) with wife Anne (d. 31 Aug 1804, 82). The inscription demonstrates literacy and resources adequate for carved stone, and it fixes the filial tie from Luke to Thomas (¹).

From Martry to Liscarton. By mid‑century, Thomas Keary is tenant at Liscarton on the Earl of Essex (Winter Estate): house, offices, c.13½ acres per Griffith’s Valuation (²). Valuation Office Revision Books subsequently mark the succession “Dead → to James Keary,” proving the father‑to‑son transfer (³).

1821 resonance. The Ardbraccan (Dormstown) households record a Thomas (50) with wife Anne (35) and children Luke (7), Thomas (0), Anne (5), Bridget (2), squarely in the Durhamstown orbit, anticipating the later civil chain.

Context. Emancipation unfolded unevenly; tithe agitation and Great Famine pressures weighed on tenants. The Kearys’ continuity through these decades indicates a modest but resilient holding supported by kin labour and market access at Navan.
