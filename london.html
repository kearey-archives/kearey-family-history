VIII. The Dublin & London Offshoot (1791–Present): Migration, Anglicisation, Social Mobility

Dublin bridge (1791). Two baptisms for boys named Thomas Keary occur at Dublin in 1791: St Paul’s (COI), parents James & Mary, and St Mary’s (RC), parents James Keary & Catherine Byrne (sponsors James Dardis & Jane Nowlan). The London line demonstrably descends from the St Paul’s child by later Anglican rites and metropolitan documentation.

Emigration to London (c.1816). Thomas Kearey marries Hester (Esther) Pepler at St Anne, Soho (1819). Across records his occupations span baggage porter → gold refiner → goldsmith, consistent with mobility among urban metal trades and transport labour. The spelling stabilises as Kearey, an orthographic anglicisation shaped by registry/guild practice and Metropolitan phonetics.

Descent. Thomas Kearey (bapt. 1791) → Thomas Henry Kearey (1820–1867) m. Hannah Raybould → Alfred Kearey (1854–1917) m. Martha Sutton → Albert Edward Kearey (1889–1971; Royal Engineers) m. Elsie May Collins (1908–1969) → Terence Arthur Kearey (b. 1935). Settlement: Paddington/Kensington → Harrow.

The wartime contraction. Alfred Kearey and several sons served in WWI; Alfred and three sons (Alfred Ernest, Frederick Augustus, Sidney Reginald) died on service. The loss of skilled male labour is reflected in later household compression and female‑headed resilience.

Reading the anglicisation. The Keary → Kearey shift did not sever identity; it regularised spelling for clerks and guilds while preserving pronunciation. The London offshoot thus sits recognisably alongside the Meath farm line.
